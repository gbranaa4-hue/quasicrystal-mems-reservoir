TITLE:
Breaking point-group symmetry activates even-order computation in modal physical reservoirs

DESCRIPTION  (paste this whole block)
----------------------------------------------------------------
Physical reservoir computing uses the nonlinear dynamics of a fixed material
system to process signals, training only a simple linear readout. A common
intuition holds that geometrically "richer" substrates — quasicrystalline or
otherwise aperiodic resonator structures — should make better reservoirs. Using
a finite-element modal model of a perforated micro-plate, this work finds that
intuition is largely false: the structural richness of a quasicrystalline mode
spectrum, and of the mode-shape-determined nonlinear coupling network, is real
but computationally inert.

The one genuine advantage identified has an exact explanation. A point-group
selection rule is proved: on a substrate with the square's D4 symmetry, the
integral of the cube of each mode shape — the modal strength of the even-order
nonlinearity that alone can generate products of past inputs — vanishes
identically for every mode except the totally symmetric ones, forcing a
fraction 7/8 of modes to be "product-blind." Breaking the point symmetry (with
an aperiodic hole pattern, or simply a lower-symmetry cavity shape) lifts the
prohibition. The result is confirmed on real finite-element modes to one part
in 10^9.

The computing consequence is statistically robust but deliberately bounded: on
shallow even-order tasks in the weakly coupled regime, a symmetry-broken
substrate outperforms a symmetric one, with the advantage scaling with the
degree of symmetry breaking — a periodic but low-symmetry plate captures about
half the effect. The benefit does not extend to deep-memory tasks or standard
benchmarks (NARMA-10), a limit traced to the conserved memory–nonlinearity
budget of any reservoir. The operative design principle is symmetry breaking,
not aperiodicity, and it applies to any modal physical computer (photonic,
spin-wave, mechanical, electronic).

This deposit includes the manuscript, supplementary material (full derivation,
controls, and cross-validation), and the complete reproducible simulation code.
