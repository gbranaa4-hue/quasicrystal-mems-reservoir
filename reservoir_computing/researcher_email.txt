Dear Dr. [Name],

I'm an independent researcher and I've just posted a short preprint I think may
be of interest given your work on physical reservoir computing.

The result is a point-group selection rule: on a modal resonator with the
square's D4 symmetry, the cubic mode overlap ∫φ³ — the modal strength of the
even-order nonlinearity needed to compute products of past inputs — vanishes
exactly for every mode outside the totally symmetric representation, silencing
7/8 of modes. Breaking the point symmetry (aperiodic perforation, or just a
lower-symmetry cavity) lifts it. I prove it by group theory and confirm it on
finite-element modes to ~1e-9, and I show the computing benefit is real but
bounded — it tracks the degree of symmetry breaking, so the operative variable
is symmetry, not aperiodicity, and it doesn't survive to deep-memory tasks.

It's a modest, simulation-only result, and I've tried hard to state its limits
honestly rather than oversell it. I'd genuinely value any reaction — whether
the framing holds up, whether it overlaps something already known, or whether
it's worth pursuing experimentally (e.g. measuring ∫φ³ optomechanically).
